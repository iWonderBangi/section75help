You paid a deposit for a new kitchen. The company has gone into administration before the installation started. Or the fitter took the money, stripped out your old kitchen, and then stopped answering calls. You are left with a deposit gone, a half-demolished space, and a job that needs finishing by someone else at your own expense.

If any payment — deposit, stage payment, or balance — went on a personal credit card, Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 may cover you for the full amount owed.

## How kitchen contracts typically work

Most kitchen contracts involve staged payments:

- **Deposit** (typically 10–25%): paid to secure the contract, often at the point of signing
- **Pre-delivery payment** (often 40–50%): paid when units are manufactured or ready to dispatch
- **Balance** (the remainder): paid on or after installation is complete

Kitchen contracts also combine goods (units, worktops, appliances) and services (design, delivery, fitting). This matters because banks sometimes try to separate the two when assessing a claim — arguing that the goods were delivered even if the installation was never completed. That argument does not hold up, but it is worth knowing about before you start.

## Which payments qualify

Section 75 applies when:

- you paid at least £1 of any payment on a personal credit card
- the total contract value was more than £100 and no more than £30,000
- the supplier has breached the contract — by collapsing, failing to start, or failing to complete what was agreed

For a supply-and-fit kitchen contract, the agreement covers everything: the design, the units, the appliances, the fitting. It is one contract, not two. The card provider's liability relates to that full contract, not just the transaction you happened to pay by card.

## The complications

**Company collapsed before work started.** The clearest scenario. There is a clear breach of contract from the moment the company fails to perform. Claim the full deposit and any other payments made.

**Company started work but did not finish.** Common and still claimable. You are entitled to claim the cost of completing the job: what you paid, minus the reasonable value of any work legitimately completed. Get a written quote from another kitchen company for the remaining work — that figure is your evidence of loss.

**Units were delivered but not installed.** Banks sometimes reject these claims on the basis that "the goods were delivered." A kitchen is not just goods — it is a supply-and-fit contract. Delivering flat-pack units to your hallway without fitting them is not delivery of what was agreed. The breach is the failure to install, and the claim stands.

**Poor quality workmanship.** Harder to argue than the above, and banks apply more scrutiny. Section 75 covers services not of satisfactory quality under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, but you will need documented evidence: photographs of the defective work, a written assessment from an independent tradesperson, and correspondence with the company where you raised the problems and they failed to address them adequately.

## Evidence to gather

- the original contract, quote, or order confirmation showing the full scope of work and total price
- credit card statements showing every payment made to the company
- any email or letter confirming the administration, collapse, or failure to proceed
- photographs of the current state: what was removed, what was or was not installed, any defects
- a written quote from another company for the completion or remediation works — this is essential for calculating your loss
- any correspondence where you chased the company and received no adequate response

If the company is in administration, a search for "[company name] administrator" will usually find the official announcement within a day of the news. Note the administrator's name — it belongs in your claim letter.

## Common bank rejection reasons — and what to say

**"The goods were partially delivered."**
Delivery of units alone does not satisfy a supply-and-fit contract. The breach is the failure to install a completed kitchen, which is what you contracted and paid for. Partial delivery of components is not partial performance of the contract.

**"You should be claiming from the administrator."**
Under Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, the card provider is jointly and severally liable with the supplier. You are not required to pursue insolvency claims first. As an unsecured creditor in an administration you would typically recover very little — Section 75 gives you a direct route to your bank instead.

**"This is a workmanship dispute, not a Section 75 matter."**
Section 75 covers breaches of contract, which includes the supply of services not of satisfactory quality. If the installation is materially defective and the company has refused or been unable to put it right, that is a breach of the contract you entered into with them — and the card provider shares liability for it.

**"We can only refund what you paid on the card."**
Section 75(1) makes the card provider liable for "any claim which the debtor has against the supplier in respect of a misrepresentation or breach of contract." That extends to the full loss arising from the breach — not just the amount charged to the card.

## If your bank refuses

Issue a written rebuttal addressing their stated reasons directly. If they maintain the refusal, refer the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service within six months of their final response letter. The FOS deals with a significant number of kitchen-related Section 75 disputes and has consistently rejected the "goods partially delivered" argument where the installation contract was not fulfilled.

## Making the claim

Write to your card provider's disputes team by email or recorded post. State clearly that you are making a claim under Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974. Describe the breach, identify what was paid and what was not delivered, and attach your evidence. Include the completion quote from your new contractor — it gives the bank a precise figure to work with.

Your wedding venue has sent an email saying they are closing. The caterer's phone goes straight to voicemail and the website is offline. The photographer who took a £1,500 deposit has become unreachable.

Discovering that a wedding supplier has failed — weeks or months before the event — is genuinely awful. The financial loss compounds it: weddings involve large sums paid well in advance to businesses that often offer little formal security.

If any of those payments were made on a personal credit card, Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 may entitle you to a refund from your card provider, even if the supplier has closed down entirely.

## Which wedding payments qualify

Section 75 applies to each supplier separately. For each one, it applies when:

- you paid at least £1 of that supplier's total contract price on a personal credit card
- the total contract price with that supplier was more than £100 and no more than £30,000
- the supplier has breached their contract — by collapsing, cancelling your booking without a full refund, or failing to provide what was agreed

The £100 threshold is based on the **total contract value**, not the deposit. If you paid a £75 deposit on a £1,200 wedding dress, the total contract value is £1,200, which qualifies — as long as at least £1 of any payment to that supplier went on a credit card.

Most main wedding costs comfortably exceed £100. The most common:

| Supplier | Typical spend | Usually qualifies? |
|---|---|---|
| Venue | £3,000–£15,000 | Yes |
| Caterer | £2,000–£8,000 | Yes |
| Photographer or videographer | £1,000–£3,500 | Yes |
| Wedding dress | £800–£3,000 | Yes |
| Florist | £500–£3,000 | Yes |
| Band or DJ | £500–£2,500 | Yes |
| Transport | £200–£1,000 | Usually yes |
| Cake | £200–£800 | Yes |

Each supplier is a separate claim. If three of your suppliers have failed, you make three separate claims — one to each card you used, or all to the same card if you paid all three on the same card.

## When a supplier cancels — not just collapses

Section 75 does not require the supplier to have gone out of business. A supplier who cancels your booking and refuses to return your deposit has breached their contract. That breach gives you a right to your money back, and if the supplier refuses, Section 75 allows you to claim against your card provider instead.

This matters particularly for venues. Some close without formally entering administration, or announce they can no longer hold your date, or change the contract terms materially without your agreement. If a supplier cannot or will not deliver what you paid for, treat it as a breach and put your claim in writing.

## Evidence to gather for each supplier

- the original booking contract or written confirmation, showing the supplier name, what was agreed, the date, and the total price
- credit card statements showing every payment made to that supplier
- written confirmation of the cancellation, closure, or failure to perform — an email, a letter, or a screenshot of a website saying the business has closed
- if the supplier is in administration: the administrator's name and announcement
- if you had to pay a replacement supplier at higher cost: the receipt for those costs

Keep each supplier's documents as a separate bundle. Submit each claim separately, even if they are all going to the same bank.

## Common bank rejection reasons — and what to say

**"The deposit was under £100."**
The threshold applies to the total contract value, not the deposit. A £500 deposit on a £5,000 venue is a qualifying claim.

**"You should claim from the administrator."**
Section 75 imposes joint and several liability. You can claim against the card provider without first pursuing the administrator or waiting for the insolvency process. As an unsecured creditor in an administration you would typically recover a fraction of what you are owed, if anything. Section 75 bypasses that queue.

**"The cancellation was due to circumstances beyond the supplier's control."**
Whether a force majeure clause in the supplier's contract applies is a matter between you and the supplier. It does not extinguish the card provider's independent liability under Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act. The bank has its own statutory obligation that exists regardless of what the supplier's terms say.

**"We can only refund the amount charged to the card."**
Section 75 covers the full value of the breach, not just the card payment. If you paid a £1,000 deposit by credit card on a £6,000 venue that has now cancelled, the card provider's liability is for the full loss arising from that breach — which may be the full £6,000, depending on what you can recover elsewhere.

## If your bank refuses

Send a written rebuttal addressing their stated reasons. If they issue a further refusal, refer the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service within six months of their final response letter. The FOS regularly upholds wedding-related Section 75 claims that banks initially refused — particularly in insolvency cases and cases where a supplier cancelled and the bank tried to limit liability to the card payment only.

## Making the claim

Write to your card provider's disputes team by email or recorded post. State that you are making a claim under Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974. Describe the breach, attach your evidence, and state the amount you are claiming. Do not phone — a written record matters if you need to escalate.

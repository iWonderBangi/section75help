Your kitchen company has gone into administration. Your holiday is cancelled and the travel firm is refusing refunds. The sofa you ordered arrived badly damaged and the retailer has stopped responding. If you paid any part of any of these purchases on a credit card, you may be able to claim your money back from your credit card provider directly, even if the retailer no longer exists.

This right comes from Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974. It makes your credit card provider jointly liable with the retailer for purchases between £100 and £30,000. It is one of the most powerful consumer protections in UK law, and it is routinely under-used, partly because banks do not advertise it and partly because the claim process is not obvious.

This guide covers everything you need to know: whether you qualify, what to include in your claim, how to write to your bank, and what to do when the first response is a rejection.

## What Section 75 actually says

Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 provides that where a debtor (you) has a claim against a supplier (the retailer) under a credit agreement, the creditor (your credit card provider) is jointly and severally liable with the supplier. In plain terms: the card provider shares the same legal responsibility as the retailer. You can sue either or both.

[See the full text of Section 75 on legislation.gov.uk.](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1974/39/section/75)

The three requirements are:

1. The purchase must have been made using a regulated consumer credit agreement (a standard personal credit card qualifies; debit cards and charge cards generally do not).
2. The total price of the item or service must be between £100 and £30,000. Note: the total price, not the amount put on the card. If a £500 holiday was partly paid by credit card and partly by bank transfer, you still qualify under Section 75 as long as at least £1 was on the card.
3. The supplier must be in breach of contract or guilty of misrepresentation. This covers non-delivery, goods not matching their description, faulty goods, and businesses ceasing to trade without delivering what was promised.

## How to make a Section 75 claim

1. **Check you qualify.** Run through the eligibility checker on our homepage. If you are unsure, see the FAQ below before contacting your bank.
2. **Gather your evidence.** You will need proof of purchase, proof that something went wrong, and evidence of your attempts to resolve the issue with the retailer. See the evidence section below.
3. **Write to your credit card provider.** This must be in writing, addressed to their Section 75 disputes team. Do not rely on a phone call. Our [claim pack](/claim-pack/) includes a template letter and a guide to what to say and what to avoid.
4. **Allow eight weeks.** The Financial Conduct Authority requires card providers to respond to complaints within eight weeks. Most respond faster for straightforward cases.
5. **If the bank rejects your claim, escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service.** See our [FOS escalation guide](/guide/financial-ombudsman/) for how to do this.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does the full purchase price need to be on the credit card?

No. Section 75 applies as long as at least £1 of the purchase was made on your credit card, and the total purchase price was between £100 and £30,000. The protection covers the full amount, not just the portion charged to the card.

### Does Section 75 cover debit cards?

No. Section 75 only applies to credit cards and other regulated consumer credit agreements. If you paid by debit card, you may be able to request a chargeback instead. See our [Section 75 vs chargeback guide](/guide/section-75-vs-chargeback/) for the differences.

### What if the retailer has gone into administration?

This is one of the most common Section 75 scenarios. Administration or liquidation is treated as a deemed breach of contract, giving you a valid claim against your card provider. The retailer does not need to be trading for your Section 75 claim to succeed.

### How long do I have to make a claim?

The limitation period in England and Wales is six years from the date the cause of action arose (when the retailer failed you). In Scotland it is five years. Making your claim promptly is always advisable; banks are less receptive to very old claims even if they are technically within the limitation period.

Your bank has rejected your Section 75 claim. Their letter runs to three paragraphs, cites no law, and tells you that their decision is final. It is not. If the bank's rejection is wrong, the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) can review the decision, direct the bank to pay, and award interest on top. The service is free, it is independent of the bank, and its decisions are legally binding.

This guide explains when you can go to the FOS, how to submit your complaint, what to send them, what happens during the investigation, and what outcomes are realistic.

## When you can escalate to the FOS

You can refer your case to the Financial Ombudsman Service in two situations:

**The bank has issued a final response.** This is sometimes called a "deadlock letter." It is a formal letter from the bank stating their final position on your complaint. Once you have received it, you have six months to refer the matter to the FOS. The clock starts on the date of the letter, not the date you received it.

**Eight weeks have passed with no response.** Under FCA rules (DISP 1.6.2R), your bank must respond to a complaint within eight weeks. If eight weeks have elapsed since you submitted your Section 75 claim and you have not received either a resolution or an acknowledgement of delay, you can go to the FOS without waiting for a final response.

You must have complained to the bank first. The FOS will not consider a case that has not already been through the bank's complaints process. If you sent a Section 75 claim letter and received no acknowledgement, send a brief follow-up by recorded post confirming the original claim date, so you have evidence the complaint was submitted.

### The six-month time limit

This is the most important deadline. Under DISP 2.8.2R, the FOS will not normally consider a complaint referred more than six months after the date of the bank's final response. Mark this date in your calendar the day you receive the rejection letter. If you miss it, the FOS may still accept your case in exceptional circumstances, but do not rely on this.

The six-year limitation period under Section 75 (or five years in Scotland) is separate and relates to your underlying legal claim. It does not override the FOS's own six-month referral window. You can have a legally valid Section 75 claim and still lose the right to use the FOS if you wait too long after the bank's rejection.

## How to submit a complaint to the FOS

**Online (fastest):** financial-ombudsman.org.uk/contact-us. This is the quickest route. You complete a form, upload your documents, and receive a case reference immediately. You can track your case online.

**By post:** Financial Ombudsman Service, Exchange Tower, London, E14 9SR. Post is slower but acceptable if you prefer. Send copies only, not originals. Use recorded delivery so you have proof of submission.

**By phone:** 0800 023 4567 (free from UK mobiles and landlines). You can call to ask questions or start the process, but you will still need to submit a written complaint with your supporting documents.

If you purchased the Section 75 Claim Pack from this site, the FOS complaint letter template (Document 8 in the pack) is pre-formatted for FOS submission and covers all the eligibility confirmation points the FOS expects to see.

## What evidence to include

Send copies of every document that tells the story of your claim. The FOS investigator needs to understand what happened, what you claimed, and why the bank's rejection is wrong, without having to ask you for anything twice. A complete submission is processed faster than an incomplete one.

**Documents relating to the original purchase:**
- Order confirmation or receipt from the retailer
- Credit card statement showing the transaction
- Any communications from the retailer (delivery confirmations, cancellation notices, administrator's letter)
- Evidence of the specific breach: administration announcement, photographs of faulty goods, comparison between the product advertised and what was delivered

**Documents relating to your Section 75 claim:**
- Copy of your original claim letter to the bank, with proof of postage if you have it
- The bank's rejection letter or letters
- Any rebuttal letters you sent after the initial rejection
- The bank's response to those rebuttals

**Confirmation of your eligibility to complain:**
- The bank's final response letter (the FOS needs to confirm you have been through the bank's process)
- Confirmation that you are the named account holder

Do not send original documents. If the FOS requires an original, they will ask. Everything else should be copies.

## What happens after you submit

### Initial triage

The FOS will check that your complaint meets their jurisdiction criteria: that you have complained to the bank first, that you are within the six-month referral window, and that the bank is regulated by the FCA. This usually takes a few weeks. You will receive a letter or email confirming your case has been accepted and assigning it to a caseworker.

### Investigation

A caseworker (called an investigator at the FOS) is assigned to your case. They will contact both you and the bank to gather information. The bank has a set period to provide their file and their reasoning.

The investigator reviews the evidence and forms a provisional view. They will write to you with this view and invite both sides to respond. If the bank agrees with the investigator's view, the case closes on those terms without needing to go further. If either side disagrees, the case is referred to an ombudsman for a formal determination.

### Ombudsman determination

An ombudsman (a senior decision-maker at the FOS) reviews the case independently. They issue a formal decision. This decision is binding on the bank if you accept it. You are not bound by it: if you disagree, you can still pursue the matter in court. Banks very rarely reject FOS decisions in practice.

## How long does it take?

The FOS is honest about its backlogs. Current typical timeframes (as published on the FOS website) are:

- **Simple, straightforward cases:** three to six months from referral to resolution
- **Cases that go to an ombudsman:** nine to eighteen months is realistic, sometimes longer

The FOS prioritises cases involving financial hardship. If the amount in dispute represents significant hardship for you (for example, you cannot pay rent or buy food as a result of the loss), say so in your complaint. This can accelerate your case.

Do not interpret slow progress as a bad sign. The FOS works through large volumes of cases and a delay is usually a queue, not a signal about the outcome.

## What the FOS can order the bank to do

If the FOS upholds your complaint, they can direct the bank to:

**Pay your claim in full.** The FOS can order the bank to pay the full amount you claimed under Section 75, up to the statutory maximum.

**Pay interest.** The FOS routinely awards interest on money the bank should have paid. The standard rate applied is 8 per cent per annum, calculated from the date of your original claim or from the date of the loss, depending on the circumstances. For a claim of £5,000 over eighteen months, this adds approximately £600.

**Pay for distress and inconvenience.** Where the bank's handling of your complaint has caused significant distress (for example, they repeatedly lost your correspondence, or they gave you inaccurate information that led you to miss a deadline), the FOS can award compensation of up to £500. This is not automatic and requires specific evidence of impact.

**Issue a written apology.** In less egregious cases, the FOS may require the bank to write formally acknowledging their error, particularly where the financial loss has been resolved but the complaint handling was poor.

The FOS cannot award punitive damages, cannot require the bank to change their policies, and cannot force a bank to accept a claim that is not legally supported. Their role is to put you back in the position you would have been in if the bank had handled the claim correctly.

## What if the FOS rejects your complaint?

The FOS upholds a significant proportion of Section 75 complaints where the bank has incorrectly applied the law, but not every escalated case succeeds. If the FOS investigator's view goes against you, read their reasoning carefully before accepting it. You have the right to request that an ombudsman reviews the case, and some investigator-level provisional views are overturned at ombudsman stage.

If the ombudsman's final decision goes against you, your remaining option is the courts. A county court claim (small claims track for amounts up to £10,000 in England and Wales) is a separate route that does not require you to have gone through the FOS first, though going through the FOS first can be useful in gathering the bank's reasoning. Taking a case to court after an FOS rejection is harder than before one, because the bank will cite the FOS decision. This route is beyond the scope of this guide.

## Why the FOS rejects Section 75 complaints: common reasons

These are the investigator-level reasons that commonly cause Section 75 escalations to fail at FOS, so you can address them in advance:

**The claim was against a debit card, not a credit card.** The FOS has no power to direct a bank under Section 75 if the card used was a debit card. Chargeback is the relevant route in that case.

**The complaint was referred out of time.** Six months from the final response letter is an absolute rule in the vast majority of cases.

**There is no evidence of a breach of contract.** Section 75 requires a breach of contract by the supplier. If all you can show is that you changed your mind, or that the supplier's terms entitled them to keep your deposit, the FOS cannot help you.

**The purchase was made through a business credit card.** Section 75 applies to regulated consumer credit agreements. Credit cards issued to limited companies do not qualify. Sole traders using a personal credit card do qualify.

**The total purchase price was outside the qualifying range (more than £100 and no more than £30,000).** The FOS will check this against the documentation.

If your case avoids all of these, and the bank's rejection was based on a misreading of the law (such as claiming they are only liable for the amount on the card, or that Section 75 does not apply to overseas purchases), your position at FOS is strong.

## Checklist before you submit

- You have received a final response letter from the bank, or eight weeks have passed since your complaint.
- You are within six months of the date on the bank's final response letter.
- You are the named account holder.
- You have copies of all relevant documents.
- You have not previously referred this complaint to the FOS.
- You have not started court proceedings against the bank in relation to this matter.

If all of these apply, you are ready to submit.

You paid a £600 deposit for a kitchen extension. The builder took the money and disappeared before starting work. You call your bank. The person on the phone mentions Section 75 in one breath and chargeback in the next, and you hang up none the wiser about which applies or whether you even need to choose.

This is one of the most common points of confusion in consumer rights. Section 75 and chargeback both let you recover money through your card provider, but they are very different in how they work, what they cover, and how strong your position is. Getting this distinction wrong can cost you money, or mean you miss a valid claim.

## What is Section 75?

Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 makes your credit card provider jointly liable with the retailer for qualifying purchases. It is a legal right, not a policy. If the retailer has breached their contract with you or misrepresented what they were selling, you can claim against the card provider directly, even if the retailer no longer exists.

The purchase must have a total price between £100 and £30,000. You only need to have put £1 on the credit card. Debit cards, charge cards, and payment services such as PayPal do not qualify.

## What is chargeback?

Chargeback is a process built into the rules of card schemes such as Visa, Mastercard, and Amex. When you dispute a transaction, your bank can reverse it and reclaim the money from the retailer's bank. It covers both credit and debit cards.

Chargeback is not a legal right in the same way as Section 75. Whether it succeeds depends on the card scheme rules and your bank's willingness to pursue it. Banks are generally cooperative for clear-cut cases (fraud, non-delivery), but they have more discretion to refuse than they do with Section 75.

The main practical disadvantage of chargeback is the time limit: Visa and Mastercard both impose a 120-day limit from the transaction date (or from the expected delivery date for goods not delivered). Some banks interpret this generously, but it is significantly shorter than Section 75's six-year limit.

## When to use Section 75

Use Section 75 if:

- You paid on a **credit card**.
- The total purchase price was **between £100 and £30,000**.
- The retailer has **breached their contract** with you or **misrepresented** what they were selling.
- You are within six years of the problem arising (England/Wales) or five years (Scotland).

Section 75 is the stronger protection. It is a statutory right, the time limit is much longer, and if the bank refuses you can escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service, which upholds a significant proportion of Section 75 complaints that banks initially reject.

## When to use chargeback

Use chargeback if:

- You paid by **debit card** (Section 75 does not apply).
- The purchase was **under £100** (below the Section 75 threshold).
- You need to act quickly within the **120-day window**.
- The dispute involves **unauthorised fraud** on your account (chargebacks for fraud are generally processed faster and more readily than Section 75 claims).

## Can you use both?

Yes, and sometimes it makes sense to try. If you paid partly by credit card and partly by debit card, you could pursue Section 75 for the credit card portion and chargeback for the debit card portion. However, you generally cannot recover more than your total loss.

If you are within the chargeback window and the amount is under £100, chargeback is your main option. If you are outside the chargeback window but within the Section 75 limitation period, and you paid on a credit card, Section 75 is your route.

## What to do if your bank conflates the two

Banks sometimes handle Section 75 claims as if they were chargebacks. This happens more often than it should, and it matters because the two processes have very different rules. Common signs that a bank is handling your Section 75 claim as a chargeback:

- They tell you the claim is outside the "dispute window" (they are applying the 120-day chargeback time limit to a Section 75 claim, which has a six-year limitation period in England and Wales).
- They say the outcome is "subject to Visa's decision" or "subject to Mastercard's rules" (card scheme rules govern chargebacks, not Section 75).
- They offer you a partial refund based only on the amount charged to the card rather than the full contract value (a chargeback recovers only what was transacted; Section 75 covers the full contract value).
- They say the claim is "discretionary" (Section 75 is not discretionary; it is a statutory right under the Consumer Credit Act 1974).

**How to correct it in writing**

Write to the bank's disputes team stating explicitly that you are making a claim under Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, not a chargeback request under card scheme rules. Keep the wording direct:

> "I am writing to confirm that my claim is a statutory claim under Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, not a chargeback request. The two processes are legally distinct. Section 75 imposes joint and several liability on you as the creditor for the full value of the contract. The 120-day card scheme dispute window does not apply to Section 75 claims. I am within the six-year limitation period applicable to this claim under the Limitation Act 1980."

Send this by email or recorded post. If the bank continues to handle your claim as a chargeback after receiving this correction, include it in your FOS complaint as evidence of incorrect procedure. The FOS takes a dim view of banks that deliberately or negligently misapply Section 75.

One practical note: if you are within the 120-day chargeback window, you do not need to choose. You can ask the bank to process both simultaneously. In practice most banks will not run the two in parallel, but it is worth asking, especially if the chargeback route is faster for your situation.

## Real cases where the distinction mattered

These scenarios are drawn from patterns in published Financial Ombudsman Service decisions. They are illustrative examples, not verbatim case summaries.

**The debit card trap.** A consumer booked a £2,400 holiday through a travel agent, paying the deposit by debit card and the balance by credit card two months later. The travel operator collapsed six weeks before departure. She attempted to claim the full £2,400 under Section 75. Her bank correctly explained that Section 75 applied only to the credit card portion of the transaction, and processed a chargeback for the debit card deposit separately. The chargeback succeeded for the deposit; the Section 75 claim covered the balance. She recovered the full amount, but only because she had split the payment across two card types and pursued both routes simultaneously. Had she paid everything by debit card, she would have been limited to chargeback and would have needed to act within 120 days.

**The expired chargeback window.** A consumer purchased a £3,500 bespoke sofa, paying £350 as a deposit by credit card and the balance of £3,150 by bank transfer five months before delivery. The furniture company ceased trading four months after the order was placed, five months after the original deposit charge. By the time the consumer contacted their bank, the 120-day chargeback window had closed on the deposit. However, because the deposit was paid by credit card and the total contract value was £3,500 (between £100 and £30,000), Section 75 applied to the full contract value, not just the £350 deposit. The bank initially rejected the claim, arguing its liability was limited to the £350 deposit and that the chargeback window had passed. The consumer escalated to the FOS, which directed the bank to pay the full £3,500. The distinction between the two processes, specifically that Section 75 covers the full contract value regardless of how much was put on the card, was the entire basis of the recovery.

**Overseas purchase, chargeback refused.** A consumer booked a villa in Portugal for £4,200, paid in full by credit card through a UK-based travel platform. The platform went into administration before the booking could be honoured. The bank initially refused both a chargeback (on the grounds that the merchant was overseas and the dispute was not fraud-related) and a Section 75 claim (on the grounds that Section 75 "does not apply to overseas suppliers"). Both rejections were wrong. On escalation to the FOS, the bank was directed to pay under Section 75. The House of Lords confirmed in OFT v Lloyds TSB Bank plc [2007] UKHL 48 that Section 75 applies to overseas purchases where a UK-regulated credit agreement was used. The bank's overseas exception argument had no basis in law. Chargeback, by contrast, is subject to scheme rules that can include geographic restrictions; Section 75 is not.

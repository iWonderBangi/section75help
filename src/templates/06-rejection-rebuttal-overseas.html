I do not accept this determination. The position you have stated is not a correct statement of the law.

Section 75 applies to overseas purchases on UK credit cards

The question of whether Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 applies to overseas transactions on UK-issued credit cards was conclusively determined by the House of Lords in Office of Fair Trading v Lloyds TSB Bank plc [2007] UKHL 48.

In that case, the banks argued that the debtor-creditor-supplier link required by Section 12(b) of the Act could not be established for overseas transactions because the credit extended by the UK card provider could not be said to finance a transaction with a specific supplier. The House of Lords rejected this argument. Their Lordships held that the debtor-creditor-supplier agreement exists by virtue of the arrangements between the card issuer and the card scheme (Visa, Mastercard, etc.), which enable cardholders to purchase from any participating supplier worldwide. The debtor-creditor-supplier link is established at the time the card is issued, not at the time of any individual transaction.

The consequence is that Section 75 applies to any purchase made on a UK-regulated credit card, regardless of whether the supplier is located in the United Kingdom or abroad.

Application to my claim

My credit card is a UK-regulated consumer credit agreement issued by your organisation. The purchase from [SUPPLIER OR COMPANY NAME] in [COUNTRY] was made using this card. The total purchase price was £[TOTAL CONTRACT PRICE] (or equivalent), which falls within the statutory range of more than £100 and not exceeding £30,000. The supplier has breached their contract with me as set out in my original claim letter dated [DATE OF ORIGINAL CLAIM].

All conditions of Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 are satisfied, and the OFT v Lloyds TSB decision confirms that the overseas nature of the transaction is not a bar to my claim.

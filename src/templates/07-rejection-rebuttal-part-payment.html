Formal Rebuttal — Section 75 Claim — Full Contract Value — Consumer Credit Act 1974

Dear Sir or Madam,

I refer to your letter dated [DATE OF REJECTION LETTER] concerning my Section 75 claim for a purchase from [SUPPLIER OR COMPANY NAME] dated [DATE OF PURCHASE].

The total purchase price was £[TOTAL CONTRACT PRICE]. Of this amount, £[AMOUNT PAID ON CREDIT CARD] was charged to my credit card with your organisation. The remaining £[AMOUNT PAID BY OTHER MEANS] was paid by [OTHER PAYMENT METHOD — e.g. "bank transfer" / "cash" / "debit card"].

You have stated that [QUOTE OR SUMMARISE THE BANK'S POSITION — e.g. "your liability under Section 75 is limited to the amount of £[AMOUNT] charged to the credit card" / "we are only responsible for the portion of the purchase paid by credit card"].

This is not a correct statement of the law.

Section 75 covers the full contract value

Section 75(1) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 provides that the creditor is "jointly and severally liable" with the supplier in respect of a "like claim" to that which the debtor has against the supplier. The claim I have against the supplier is for the full purchase price of £[TOTAL CONTRACT PRICE] — that is, the full value of the contract that has been breached. This is my "like claim" against you as the jointly liable creditor.

The requirement in Section 75 that a credit card is used is a qualifying condition for liability, not a cap on the amount of that liability. Section 75(3)(b) makes clear that the qualifying threshold relates to the cash price of the item or service, not the amount financed by credit. The statute requires only that the credit card was used for part of the transaction, not that it was used for all of it.

This interpretation is consistent with the way the Financial Ombudsman Service handles such cases. The FOS routinely awards the full contract value in Section 75 claims where only part of the price was paid by credit card.

The threshold condition is satisfied

The relevant threshold under Section 75(3)(b) is that the cash price of the goods or services exceeds £100 and does not exceed £30,000. The cash price in this case is £[TOTAL CONTRACT PRICE], which satisfies this condition. The threshold applies to the total price of the item, not to the amount charged to the credit card.

My position

I am entitled to claim the full contract value of £[TOTAL CONTRACT PRICE] under Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, less any amounts already recovered from other sources. My outstanding claim is for £[TOTAL AMOUNT CLAIMED].

I ask that you revise your determination and issue a new final response within 14 days of this letter, upholding my claim for the full amount.

If you maintain the position that Section 75 limits your liability to the amount charged to the credit card only, I will refer this matter to the Financial Ombudsman Service, which I expect will uphold my claim in accordance with the established interpretation of Section 75(1) and Section 75(3)(b).
